A talk on computer-assisted fieldwork

AI as a scout,
not a judge.

Building a Southern Kurdish dialect tree with PARSE.

Lucas Ardelean · University of Bamberg · 2026

The classification puzzle

Sixty years of disagreement
over a single dialect group.

  • 11 speakers, 6 Southern Kurdish varieties — Faili, Kalhori, Khanaqini, Qasri, Mandali, Sahana
  • Iraq–Iran borderlands — a shatter zone of tribal, political, and contact pressure
  • MacKenzie (1961), Fattah (2000), Belelli (2019), Mohammadirad (forthcoming) — no consensus
  • Isoglosses cross-cut each other; no clean tree by traditional methods

Why a "press the button" AI does not work here

Three problems. One job.

  • Dialect-diagnostic vowels go missing. Southern Kurdish distinguishes itself with the fronted rounded vowels /œ/ and /ʏ/. Mainstream speech recognition, trained on European languages, collapses exactly those contrasts.
  • Borrowing looks like cognacy. Basic vocabulary is shot through with Arabic and Persian loans. A machine treats waqt as a shared word across varieties — phonetically it is; historically it is not.
  • Fieldwork is rich; this question only needs a slice. 11 speakers × 2.5–5 h of recording. 530+ words elicited per speaker; only 85 are phylogenetically necessary. The other 445+ are valuable for other studies — phonology, syntax, sociolinguistic variation — just not what a phylogeny is asking.
  • The job is not "transcribe everything." The job is to find the right 85, in the right order, with the right confidence, inside hours of speech where the elicitation sequence drifts.

A guiding principle

The machine narrows the search. The linguist makes the judgment.

I.

AI scouts

It locates likely regions in long recordings and offers a candidate transcription.

II.

The linguist judges

Every accept, edit, or rejection is an explicit human action.

III.

Nothing saves itself

The system never commits a transcription or a cognate decision on its own.

IV.

The trail stays visible

Computed result and human correction are stored as separate layers — auditable forever.

— after Computer-Assisted Language Comparison (List 2016)

Job 1 · Locating

Finding 85 needles
in 530+ words of haystack.

  • The pipeline locates — it does not transcribe.
  • Speech recognition: a Whisper model fine-tuned for Southern Kurdish, returning Kurdish-script words with word-level timestamps
  • Voice activity detection retuned so a 5-second prompt–response gap is not glued into one utterance
  • Output: a ranked list of candidate audio regions — never a committed transcript

Job 2 · Cross-checking

A second opinion
on every IPA string.

  • A second model reads the same audio independently as phonemes — not words
  • Its IPA output is compared against my manual IPA, character by character
  • Big disagreements get flagged for re-listening
  • Purpose: catch drift in my own conventions across months of fieldwork — not replace expert transcription

Job 3 · Anchoring

The dataset becomes
its own map.

  • Once a handful of words are verified, their positions form a map of the elicitation
  • Missing words can be predicted to fall in a narrow time window
  • Repetitions (each item said 2–4 times) detected as clusters within 30 seconds
  • Cross-speaker matching: seven verified "hand"s make the eighth easier
  • Gets faster as the dataset grows — not magic, just more reference points

Job 4 · Adjudicating

Proposing cognate groups —
and where they go wrong.

  • LexStat derives sound-correspondence patterns from the data itself — better than universal edit distance
  • Got this wrong: grouped Arabic waqt ("time") as cognate across varieties — a shared borrowing, not shared inheritance
  • Missed this: failed to link dast and das ("hand") — final-cluster deletion looked like a mismatch
  • Both fixed by hand; both corrections preserved alongside the algorithm's output

PARSE — the workstation that keeps the human in charge

Two modes, one underlying dataset.

  • Original audio never cut. Every annotation is a pointer with timestamps — archival integrity preserved.
  • Two layers stored. Computed result and human correction live side-by-side — every decision is reproducible.
  • Open formats out. LingPy TSV + NEXUS feed straight into the BEAST 2 phylogenetic stage.

What this gets me — and what it doesn't

The honest ledger.

Delivers

  • 30 hours of audio reduced to a verified 85-concept × 11-speaker matrix
  • Every cell listened to and approved by a human
  • Audit trail of where the algorithm and the linguist disagreed
  • A Bayesian distribution over candidate trees — uncertainty is part of the result, not an embarrassment

Does not claim

  • The AI adjudicates dialect membership
  • The AI decides what is a cognate
  • The AI transcribes IPA for publication
  • The classification answer comes from the machine

Why this might matter for language assessment

In assessment, the rater's judgment is the result. Audit trails and second opinions matter more than raw accuracy.

The same pattern — scout, second opinion, human-owned decision, full provenance — applies wherever expert judgment doesn't scale. Proficiency rating, error coding, dictation, oral-task transcription. The tool may not transfer; the discipline does.
